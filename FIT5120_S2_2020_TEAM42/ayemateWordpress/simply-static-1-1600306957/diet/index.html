Overview

There’s a lot of focus on what a nutritious diet can do for children’s physical health, like proper growth, stronger bones, and even a lower risk for chronic disease in adulthood. But what about their mental health? Improving mental health might initially seem like it has nothing to do with what we eat but there is a strong correlation between what we eat and how we feel socially and emotionally!

Poor diet can make your child feel low, lethargic and might increase the symptoms of depression and anxiety as well. On the other hand, having a nutritious diet helps you keep a healthy heart and weight that in turn reduces the risk of chronic diseases. It also helps with sleeping patterns. Human brain needs an adequate amount of nutrients to stay active.

The type of food we eat has the ability to affect our mood. So choose your diet wisely so as to lift your children’s mood, energy levels and concentration.

Poor nutrition has been associated with:

Externalising behaviour such as hyperactivity, aggression, disobedience

Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

Tiredness

Psychological stress, anxiety and depression

Delayed brain development

Cognitive function impairments associated with learning and memory

Now that you understand the importance of a healthy diet, get to know more about the fundamentals of healthy eating in the next block.

Fundamentals of healthy eating

While some extreme diets may suggest otherwise, we all need a balance of protein, fat, carbohydrates, fiber, vitamins, and minerals in our diets to sustain a healthy body. You don’t need to eliminate certain categories of food from your child’s diet, but rather select the healthiest options from each category for them:

Fat

In fact, healthy fats like omega-3s are vital to your children’s physical and emotional health. Including more healthy fat in your child’s diet can help improve your child’s mood, boost their well-being, and help them to be fit.

Calcium

Not getting enough calcium in your child’s diet can also contribute to anxiety, depression, and sleep difficulties. It’s vital to include calcium-rich foods in your child’s diet.

Carbohydrates

Most should come from complex, unrefined carbs (vegetables, whole grains, fruit) .Cutting back on white bread, pastries, starches, and sugar can prevent rapid spikes in blood sugar, fluctuations in mood and energy, and a build-up of fat.

Protein

Will help to give your child energy to cope up with their daily activities while also supporting their mood and cognitive function.

Food(s) that make anxiety worse

You already know that your emotions can influence what you eat. But what you may not realize is that what your kid’s eat can also dramatically alter your kid’s mental health.  Some foods worsen anxiety and depression for your kids.

Steer clear of these foods and ingredients that have been found to have a negative impact on individual’s mood:

 

1.

Sugar

Increase in added sugars in their diet has the ability to increase anxiety in your child.The simple act of eating sugar makes your brain work at a suboptimal level and the more you do it, the greater the risk of anxiety and the greater the risk of diabetes and dementia, too.  Limit the intake of sugar in your child’s diet

2.

Artificial Sweeteners

Don’t think that just because sugar is out that artificial sweeteners will enable you can humor your sweet tooth without elevating your risk of anxiety. Aspartame, the common ingredient that’s found in products like diet soda, blocks the production of the neurotransmitter serotonin. This can cause all manner of neuro maladies including headaches, insomnia, changes in mood—and yes, anxiety.

3.

Hydrogenated Oil

Fried chicken, fried cheese sticks, fried calamari, French fries. You won’t ever see these items marked as an “Eat This.” They cause trouble for your body for a variety of reasons and can negatively affect your weight. But there’s more: They’re also linked to anxiety. 

4.

Fast Food

Cheap and easy? In the short term, kinda sorta. But once you factor in the changes it can make to your child’s physical and mental well being, the true price of that cheap stuff gets steep real fast.

5.

Trans Fats

Trans fat is the name given to unsaturated fats that don’t usually occur in whole foods. Only in the 1950s did trans fats become commonly used in things like margarine, snack food, packaged baked goods, and oils used to fry fast food. Consuming artery-clogging trans fats can increase the risk of anxiety by as much as 48 percent.

6.

High-Sodium Foods

For decades, fat-free foods have been touted as being a weight loss solution but many of these products contain mini mountains of sodium. Experts say that all that extra salt can totally futz with your kid’s emotions because the extra sodium in these products can disrupt aspects of the neurological system. Not only can this directly contribute to anxiety, but it can also monkey with the immune system response and cause fatigue.

7.

Caffeine

There are plenty of experts who will tell you that even a modest amount of caffeine can contribute to anxiety. The reason most experts cite is the caffeine’s disruptive effect on sleep. Sleep is connected to mood and disturbed sleep can seriously mess with your kid’s mental state.

8.

Processed Foods

Processed foods are the perfect storm of several things that can be problematic to the overall health of your child. They’re high in sodium and sugar, and pave the way for an inflammatory response in the body.

9.

Tofu

Even though tofu is packed with lean protein, it’s also packed with trypsin and protease inhibitors enzymes that make the digestion of protein incredibly difficult. Soy is also high in copper, a mineral linked to anxious behavior, and loaded with oligosaccharides, which are known to cause flatulence.

10.

Wheat Bran

Touted by health experts and foodies for its impressive fiber content and complex, nutty flavor, wheat bran gets a black mark in the anti-anxiety department for its notoriously high concentration of phytic acid. This anti-nutrient binds to important mood minerals like zinc and limits their absorption.
